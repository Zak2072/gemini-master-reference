# Partnership Outreach

Manages the full workflow from target research through to a finished approach letter or email. Built primarily for CFPA corporate partnership development but applicable to any partnership or sponsorship context.

## Important Distinction

**B Corp** and **National TOMs** are separate and distinct frameworks. B Corp certification is awarded by B Lab to businesses that meet verified standards of social and environmental performance, accountability, and transparency. National TOMs is a social value measurement framework used primarily in public sector procurement. Do not conflate them. When approaching B Corp businesses, reference B Corp values and certification specifically — not National TOMs language.

## Context

The primary use case is CFPA partnership development, particularly:
- Approaching B Corp certified businesses in the Cheltenham and wider Gloucestershire area
- Centenary year (2026) partnership opportunities with a specific time-limited hook
- Corporate partnerships that offer genuine mutual value, not just a sponsorship transaction

Secondary contexts:
- Cheltenham Chamber of Commerce partnership development
- EXELA commercial partnerships
- King's Trust or other charitable partnership approaches

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Target Research

Before drafting anything, research the target organisation:

1. **Confirm B Corp status** if relevant: check the B Lab directory at `https://www.bcorporation.net/en-us/find-a-b-corp/`
2. **Identify their stated values and impact areas**: check their website, B Impact Report if available, and recent news
3. **Find the right contact**: look for CSR lead, marketing director, or senior leadership depending on company size
4. **Look for an existing connection**: does CFPA, David, or the Chamber have any prior relationship with this organisation?
5. **Identify the alignment angle**: what specifically connects this organisation's stated values to what CFPA offers?

Summarise findings in a brief before drafting:
- Company name and B Corp status (if applicable)
- Stated values most relevant to CFPA
- Right contact and their role
- Specific alignment angle
- Any prior connection

### Phase 2: Approach Strategy

Determine the approach type based on the target:

**Cold approach to a B Corp:**
Lead with genuine alignment between their certified values and CFPA's community impact. The centenary year provides a specific, time-limited hook. Avoid generic "we'd love to partner with you" language — be specific about what the partnership would look like and what it would achieve for both parties.

**Warm approach (existing connection):**
Reference the connection early but don't lean on it. The case for partnership still needs to stand on its own merits.

**Chamber or civic partnership:**
Frame around mutual benefit to the Cheltenham business community and shared civic investment. Less emphasis on brand visibility, more on community outcomes.

### Phase 3: Draft the Approach

#### Email / Letter Structure

1. **Opening**: One sentence establishing the specific reason for writing to them in particular — not a generic opener. If approaching a B Corp, this should reference their certification or a specific value they've publicly committed to.

2. **Who we are**: Two to three sentences on CFPA. Lead with the century of community impact and the centenary milestone if 2026 is still current. Specific reach and engagement data is more powerful than "Cheltenham's premier performing arts festival."

3. **The alignment**: Explain concretely why this organisation and CFPA are a natural fit. This must be specific to this organisation, not a template paragraph.

4. **What partnership looks like**: Give them a clear picture of what you are proposing — naming rights, programme advertising, community engagement events, bursary co-funding, or whatever is appropriate. Vague "exploring partnership opportunities" language invites a polite no.

5. **The ask**: Clear and specific. A meeting, a call, a response. One ask, not several.

6. **Closing**: Confident, not effusive. Brief thanks, direct sign-off.

#### Tone

- Peer register: approaching as an established cultural institution with 100 years of history, not as a supplicant asking for charity
- Specific: every generic sentence is an opportunity for the reader to disengage
- Warm but not gushing
- British English, no em dashes, no AI slop vocabulary (Text DNA rules apply)
- Length: emails should be readable in 90 seconds; letters one page maximum

### Phase 4: Follow-Up

If a follow-up is needed after no response:

- Wait at least ten working days before following up
- Reference the original email briefly but do not repeat it
- Add one new piece of information or context if possible (a centenary event date, a relevant piece of news about the festival)
- One follow-up only; a second unanswered follow-up is the signal to move on

## Principles

- Research first, always. A well-researched approach to ten targets will outperform a generic approach to a hundred.
- The centenary is a specific asset in 2026 — use it precisely, not as a vague prestige claim.
- Partnership should feel mutual. If you cannot articulate what the organisation gets from this beyond logo placement, the approach is not ready.
- Never conflate B Corp values with CSR box-ticking language. B Corp businesses have made a genuine commitment and respond to genuine alignment, not flattery.
